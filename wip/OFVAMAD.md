Good, lasting change is rarely glamorous. It does not tend to take the form of a glorious revolution. And even if it does there is always plenty more work to be done the next day. Real change, the type that makes the world a better place, always comes from the bottom up. It happens in a thousand small steps, a billion individual decisions. We may only notice it when something snaps at the top of the power structure as the big institutions suddenly catch up or rally against what has already happened. But ultimately what will have made the difference will always be many people just trying to learn and do better. 

And that is not so satisfying for an individualist. For someone born into a culture of celebrity and self. We all want to support the winner. The person who was right, and who had all the answers. Or even better, to be them. To be the person storming the ramparts with a flag in hand, triumphantly declaring permanent victory and justice for all. But such moments and such people do not exist, not really. Though it may be a truism that no one is perfect it is also an essential truth that so many of us are deceived into forgetting or making exceptions from. Evil creeps back in the moment we declare ourselves or anyone else above scrutiny or morality. 

We can't and shouldn't spend our lives looking for the perfect cause, the perfect movement the perfect person to follow. We should just strive be better. Individually and collectively. And fortunately or unfortunately, depending on your perspective, that is a task that never ends.  

And so we must be ever vigilant. Both within ourselves and without the revolution must be constant. Every day will involve trying to tread our own path, striving ever forward. And doing so, not because there is some mystical peak at which there lies a perfect us or a perfect world, but because caring and striving is what we were made to do. It is what we want to do. 

I suppose in some sense, with all this in mind we could end up viewing the anarchist cause as one which is eternally failing. Every power structure which is allowed to stand, every act of oppression acted out without consequence, every murder, rape and genocide is a moment we have lost.

But in another sense, in the sense I subscribe to, every moment of freedom which someone gets to live out is a victory. Every liberation, collective and individual is a triumph. Every time someone dances wildly, laughs openly and loves freely we have won a tremendous victory.

The communes may not last forever. And history may spend quite some time tending towards repression. There is no guarantee we will win, and certainly no guarantee when we do it will last forever. But the same applies to those who oppress also.

The goal of freedom for all is ethereal, and the task of bringing it about is necessarily endless. But that is not a burden. It is a gift of responsibility, of purpose, of meaning. The question then becomes whether you will see that gift for what it is and take every chance to honour that responsibility, or whether you will consign yourself to the guilt of a life half lived.

A friend told me recently that people need to consider what they are willing to sacrifice for being a part of making the world a better place. And I think that is true. But I think they should also keep in mind what they have to gain. Which is Everything. Love. Community. Purpose. A life worth living. Absolutely everything that was ever truly worthwhile. And I think when you consider that, all the trinkets and wonders we may indeed have to give up suddenly don’t feel all that important anyways.
