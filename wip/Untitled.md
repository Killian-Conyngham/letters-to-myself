
I had a conversation recently, with a good friend, about climate change and societal collapse. About our responsibility to do something about it, and the feelings of judgement towards those who don’t, which arise all too easily and seem to rarely serve more of a purpose than to alienate and divide. As we talked, she explained how as someone who avoids flying she can feel jealous towards those living her 16-year old dream-life of flying around the world. People who deprioritise the collective good in favour of their own happiness. And I realised, as she was talking that I could relate so very strongly, just not on the topic of climate change.

  

She described how it is so hard to see those people live out their lives because deep down some part of her wants to do those things too, but can’t because her morals and obligations and desire to be a positive force in the world inhibit her from doing so. And all I could think about was the moments of jealousy I experienced across my experimentations with non-monogamy in my last relationships. Because the sentiment she was expressing captured the feelings so well.  

And I had to think then of the most revelatory moment. The

  

I rarely judge others for flying. Not because some part of my doesn’t want to fly. I know that part of me exists. (And to be fair, I have been on 5 flights since I made the commitment 5 years ago). It’s also not because I don’t think unnecessary flying is a bad thing to do.

  

I don’t judge others for flying, simply because I find the part of myself that wants to fly quite easy to ignore. I know how much joy has come to my life as a result of taking the long route. I know having been given so much by life already, excess flying would only make me feel guilt and discontent. I know that any pleasure I imagine from the experience pales in comparison to the contentment of living my life authentically and honest to my morals. And I know, most importantly, that arriving with judgement or derision only decimates the chances of an open honest exchange that might change minds for the better.  

  

And I find it easy to be objective about all that, precisely because it feels like a settled topic. None of my personal insecurities rest there. At this current moment at least.
